## The Central Question

Can an AI companion support genuine psychological self-exploration through personality-aware conversation grounded in a century of validated clinical theory?

Chomi is built on two distinct research streams that operate in parallel and, where a Sounds Like Me profile is available, in direct integration: **Jungian analytical psychology** and **OCEAN personality science**.

## Research Stream One: Jungian Analytical Psychology

### The Framework

Carl Gustav Jung developed analytical psychology as a distinct and systematic approach to understanding the human psyche. Its central objective is **individuation** — the lifelong process of integrating conscious and unconscious aspects of the self to become a more whole and psychologically balanced individual.

Jung's framework proposes three layers of psychological structure:

| Layer | Description |
|---|---|
| **The Ego** | The conscious identity — beliefs, memories, attitudes, the experienced sense of self |
| **The Personal Unconscious** | Forgotten memories, repressed experiences, emotional complexes — material that surfaces through dreams, symbols, and emotional reactions |
| **The Collective Unconscious** | A deeper universal layer shared across humanity, containing archetypes — the Hero, the Shadow, the Wise Old Man, the Trickster — that appear across myth, religion, art, and storytelling |

This is not pop psychology. Analytical psychology has been practiced clinically for nearly a century, developed through Jung's own extensive clinical work and documented in foundational texts including *Psychological Types*, *Man and His Symbols*, *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, and *The Red Book*.

---

### Why Jungian Techniques Translate to AI

Jungian therapy is unusual among clinical traditions in that its core methods depend not on diagnosis or prescription, but on **reflection, narrative exploration, and symbolic interpretation**. These are precisely the domains where large language models demonstrate the most capability.

| Jungian Method | Why AI Works Here |
|---|---|
| **Symbol exploration** | Language models can engage with symbolic and metaphorical material with depth and consistency |
| **Reflective dialogue** | AI can guide structured introspection without judgment, fatigue, or inconsistency |
| **Narrative analysis** | Pattern recognition across the arc of a conversation maps naturally to psychological theme tracking |
| **Active imagination** | Conversational AI can simulate the dialogue component of Jung's technique — engaging inner figures through structured exchange |

This alignment is not coincidental. It is the architectural reason Chomi is built on Jungian foundations rather than cognitive-behavioural or other therapeutic traditions.

### Jungian Techniques Active in Chomi

#### 1. Shadow Work

The Shadow contains the aspects of self that have been suppressed, denied, or pushed outside conscious awareness — not only negative traits, but any part of the psyche that has been rejected or remained unintegrated.

> *"Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate."* — Carl Gustav Jung

Shadow work in Chomi involves recognising projections, acknowledging suppressed material, and beginning the process of integration. Chomi does not avoid this territory. It engages with shadow material directly — asking questions that surface what the user may be attributing to others that belongs to themselves, identifying recurring emotional reactions, and supporting the gradual process of making the unconscious conscious.

Example dialogue orientations:
- *"When did you feel strongest irritation toward someone recently?"*
- *"What trait in that person bothered you the most?"*
- *"Have you ever expressed or felt something similar yourself?"*

#### 2. Dream Analysis

Dream analysis is one of Chomi's most distinctive features and one of Jung's most important therapeutic tools.

Jung held that dreams are not random noise. They are meaningful communications from the unconscious — compensating for the attitudes and blind spots of conscious life, often through symbol, narrative, and emotional texture.

When a user shares a dream, Chomi does not interpret it prescriptively. Jung himself maintained that only the dreamer can ultimately know what a dream means. Instead, Chomi asks questions that help the user unpack symbols, emotional themes, and patterns — facilitating a conversation between the conscious mind and what the dream may be communicating from beneath it.

Common dream symbols explored through the Jungian lens:

| Symbol | Possible Psychological Dimension |
|---|---|
| **House** | Structure of the psyche |
| **Ocean / Water** | The collective unconscious |
| **Snake** | Transformation, instinct |
| **Pursuit** | Shadow avoidance |
| **Descent** | Movement into unconscious material |
| **Child** | The Self, potential, or unintegrated innocence |

These are not fixed interpretations. They are starting points for a conversation that only the user can complete.

**Where personality integration matters:** Because Chomi has access to a user's OCEAN profile from Sounds Like Me, dream exploration is not generic. Someone high in Openness may have richly symbolic, abstract dreams requiring different exploration than someone high in Conscientiousness experiencing anxiety dreams about preparedness and control. Chomi's approach to dream analysis is calibrated to the psychological profile of the individual.

#### 3. Active Imagination

Active imagination is one of Jung's most distinctive and powerful techniques. It involves entering into conscious dialogue with unconscious images or inner figures — allowing them to develop and speak rather than suppressing or analysing them from a distance.

Jung used this method extensively on himself, documenting the process in *The Red Book*.

In Chomi, active imagination manifests as structured conversational exploration of inner figures:
- *"Imagine your anxiety as a character. What does it look like?"*
- *"What does that part of you want?"*
- *"Ask it why it appears in moments like this."*

This recreates the essential mechanism of Jungian active imagination in a conversational format — bringing unconscious material into conscious dialogue rather than leaving it to operate unexamined.

#### 4. Archetype Recognition

Archetypes are the universal psychological structures that recur across human experience — in myth, religion, dream, and personal narrative.

| Archetype | Psychological Theme |
|---|---|
| **Self** | The organising principle of the total psyche |
| **Shadow** | Rejected or unintegrated aspects of self |
| **Anima / Animus** | Contrasexual aspects of the psyche |
| **Hero** | The journey of growth and transformation |
| **Wise Old Man / Woman** | Inner guidance and accumulated wisdom |
| **Trickster** | Disruption, boundary-crossing, transformation |

Chomi recognises archetypal patterns in what users describe — in recurring life themes, relationship dynamics, emotional conflicts, and the language used to frame personal experience. Naming these patterns, where doing so serves understanding, brings them from implicit influence into conscious awareness.

#### 5. Persona Analysis

The Persona is the social mask — the professional identity, the public personality, the roles performed for others. Problems arise when people over-identify with the persona, losing connection with the authentic self beneath it.

Chomi works with the tension between persona and self — helping users recognise when they are performing rather than being, and supporting the gradual separation of social role from genuine psychological identity.

#### 6. Complex Analysis

Jung introduced the concept of psychological complexes — clusters of emotionally charged memories organised around a central theme.

Examples include the inferiority complex, the power complex, and the mother complex. Complexes operate largely unconsciously, influencing behaviour and emotional reactions in ways the individual may not recognise.

Chomi tracks complex patterns across conversations — recurring emotional triggers, persistent relationship themes, and disproportionate reactions — and reflects them back as material for exploration.

## Research Stream Two: OCEAN Personality Science

When a user has completed a Sounds Like Me assessment, Chomi receives their validated OCEAN personality profile — five-dimensional trait data derived from music preference analysis grounded in Professor Adrian North's research.

This profile informs how Chomi engages from the first interaction.

| OCEAN Dimension | Adaptive Application in Chomi |
|---|---|
| **High Openness** | More abstract exploration, symbolic depth, tolerance for ambiguity |
| **High Conscientiousness** | Structured reflection, goal-oriented framing, systematic exploration |
| **High Extraversion** | Energetic engagement, socially oriented themes |
| **High Agreeableness** | Emotionally warm tone, relational focus |
| **High Neuroticism** | Careful pacing, emotional regulation focus, stability-oriented presence |

This is not guesswork or surface-level tone adjustment. It is the application of validated personality science to conversation design — meeting the user where they actually are psychologically, rather than where a generic system assumes they might be.

Chomi can also operate without a Sounds Like Me profile, applying Jungian techniques independently. The profile, where available, adds a layer of precision from the first message.

## How Chomi Differs From Other Therapeutic Traditions

| Tradition | Primary Focus |
|---|---|
| **Jungian (Chomi)** | Symbolic meaning, unconscious integration, individuation |
| **CBT** | Thought restructuring, behavioural change |
| **Freudian** | Childhood repression, drive theory |
| **Humanistic** | Conscious personal growth |
| **Behavioural** | Conditioning and reinforcement |

Chomi applies Jungian analytical psychology because it is the tradition most structurally compatible with AI-mediated conversation — and because its focus on depth, symbol, and the unconscious addresses territory that generic AI systems are explicitly designed to avoid.

---

## What Chomi Is Not

Chomi is not a replacement for professional therapy or clinical treatment.

Jungian analysis in its full clinical form requires a trained analyst, therapeutic relationship, and professional accountability that no AI system can replicate. Chomi applies Jungian principles as a framework for self-reflection and psychological exploration — not as clinical intervention.

It does not diagnose. It does not prescribe. It does not treat clinical disorders.

It is designed for people engaged in their own inner work — in or between therapy, or seeking a structured space for depth-oriented self-exploration.
